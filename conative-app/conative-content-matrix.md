# Conative Operating Manual — Content Matrix

> Voice: Plain language. Direct. Second person. Short sentences. No jargon. Every line should feel like a friend who knows you well telling you how you work.

---

## PART 1: THE 12 CONATIVE STRENGTHS

Each strength gets: Name, Plain-Language Title, Description (2-3 sentences), Superpower, Shadow, "You at Your Best," "You Under Stress," and a sample "What Others Should Know About You" line.

---

### FACT FINDER — How You Gather and Share Information

---

#### FF-01: SIMPLIFY (Fact Finder 1-3)

**Plain Title:** The Big Picture Thinker

**Description:**
You cut through noise fast. While others are drowning in data, you've already pulled the one thing that matters and moved on. You don't need all the facts. You need the right facts.

**Superpower:**
You make complex things simple. You can walk into a room full of overwhelm, ask two questions, and clarify the whole situation. People leave conversations with you feeling lighter because you stripped out everything that didn't matter.

**Shadow:**
You can dismiss important details too fast. Sometimes the thing you skipped over was actually the thing that mattered. You can also frustrate people who need more evidence before they feel safe moving forward. Their need for data isn't weakness. It's a different operating system.

**You at Your Best:**
Summarizing a 40-page report into a 3-sentence decision framework. Cutting a meeting from 60 minutes to 15 by asking the one question nobody else thought to ask. Making the complicated feel obvious.

**You Under Stress:**
Oversimplifying things that actually need nuance. Dismissing research someone spent real time on. Getting impatient with people who need more information to feel confident. Mistaking speed for accuracy.

**What Others Should Know:**
"Don't give me the whole history. Give me the headline and what you need from me. I'll ask if I need more."

---

#### FF-02: EXPLAIN (Fact Finder 4-6)

**Plain Title:** The Translator

**Description:**
You take in the right amount of information for the situation and translate it so others can act on it. You don't need every detail, but you don't wing it either. You give people exactly what they need, no more, no less.

**Superpower:**
You match the depth of your research to the actual demands of the situation. You clarify without overloading. You write clear instructions, build useful summaries, and bridge the gap between the person who knows everything and the person who knows nothing.

**Shadow:**
You can drift. Without a strong pull toward either deep research or radical simplification, you sometimes do "enough" research without being sure if it was actually enough. The middle ground is flexible, but it can also feel vague when precision matters.

**You at Your Best:**
Editing a document so it says more with fewer words. Explaining a complex process to someone with zero background, and watching them get it immediately. Reviewing priorities and knowing exactly which details to keep.

**You Under Stress:**
Second-guessing whether you researched enough. Spending energy translating for others when nobody asked. Feeling caught between people who want more data and people who want less.

**What Others Should Know:**
"I'll give you what's relevant. If you want the deep dive, tell me. If you want the summary, I'll give you that too. Just tell me what level we're working at."

---

#### FF-03: SPECIFY (Fact Finder 7-10)

**Plain Title:** The Deep Researcher

**Description:**
You don't guess. You don't estimate. You go deep. Before you commit to anything, you've already gathered more data than most people knew existed. You trust evidence over intuition, and your confidence comes from knowing you've done the work.

**Superpower:**
You catch what everyone else misses. Your thoroughness protects teams from bad decisions built on assumptions. You bring receipts. When you present a recommendation, it's backed by real data, real precedent, and real analysis. People trust your conclusions because they know how you got there.

**Shadow:**
You can over-research. The feeling of "not enough information" can keep you digging when the team needed a decision three days ago. You can also overwhelm others with the depth of your findings when they only needed the top-line answer. Depth is your gift. Knowing when to stop is your growth edge.

**You at Your Best:**
Building a case so thorough that the decision becomes obvious. Finding the one data point nobody else found that changes the whole strategy. Defining exactly what a word means when everyone else was using it loosely.

**You Under Stress:**
Analysis paralysis. Refusing to commit until you have 100% certainty (which never comes). Burying teammates in details they didn't ask for. Getting frustrated when others make decisions based on "gut feel."

**What Others Should Know:**
"If I'm asking a lot of questions, I'm not doubting you. I'm building confidence in the decision. Give me the data and I'll give you the best answer I can."

---

### FOLLOW THRU — How You Organize and Design Systems

---

#### FT-01: ADAPT (Follow Thru 1-3)

**Plain Title:** The Shortcut Finder

**Description:**
You don't follow the process. You route around it. While others are checking boxes, you've already found the faster way. You work in piles, not files. You thrive in chaos that would paralyze a structured thinker.

**Superpower:**
You're the most flexible person in the room. Plans change? You're already adjusting. System breaks? You're already finding the workaround. You can juggle multiple projects in various stages of done without losing your mind. Your brain is wired for improvised structure, not imposed structure.

**Shadow:**
Following through on someone else's process physically drains you. You can leave things 80% finished because the last 20% is boring. Your "system" makes perfect sense to you and zero sense to anyone else. You can also frustrate organized teammates who need things in a specific place.

**You at Your Best:**
Handling five interruptions in a row and still producing. Finding the shortcut through a bureaucratic nightmare. Adapting a broken plan on the fly without missing a beat. Keeping a dozen plates spinning.

**You Under Stress:**
Losing track of commitments because they weren't written down anywhere. Leaving important things unfinished. Resisting any process that feels rigid, even when rigidity would actually help. Creating confusion for teammates who can't follow your system (because it's in your head).

**What Others Should Know:**
"Don't give me a 47-step checklist. Give me the outcome you need and let me find my way there. I'll get it done. It just won't look like your way."

---

#### FT-02: MAINTAIN (Follow Thru 4-6)

**Plain Title:** The System Keeper

**Description:**
You don't build new systems from scratch, and you don't ignore them either. You keep what works running. You adjust, tweak, and tune. When something's almost right, you're the one who makes it actually right.

**Superpower:**
You bridge the gap between the person who creates the system and the person who breaks it. You spot inconsistencies, clean up rough edges, and keep things running smoothly without needing total control. You're comfortable with someone else's framework as long as there's room to adjust it.

**Shadow:**
You might not speak up when a whole system needs to be rebuilt, because tweaking feels safer than replacing. You can also undervalue your contribution because "keeping things running" doesn't feel as impressive as "built this from scratch."

**You at Your Best:**
Taking a disorganized project and quietly bringing order to it. Spotting the one broken step in a process that everyone else missed. Keeping a team coordinated without being controlling about it.

**You Under Stress:**
Maintaining a system that should've been scrapped. Feeling invisible because your work is "behind the scenes." Tolerating too much chaos before finally saying something.

**What Others Should Know:**
"I'll keep us organized. Don't mistake my flexibility for not caring about structure. I just don't need to own the whole system to keep it working."

---

#### FT-03: SYSTEMATIZE (Follow Thru 7-10)

**Plain Title:** The System Builder

**Description:**
You need structure. Not someone else's structure. Yours. You instinctively design sequential processes, chart timelines, create order from chaos, and build systems that turn vague ideas into repeatable outcomes.

**Superpower:**
You turn abstract projects into concrete plans. You're the reason things actually get finished. You create the timeline, the checklist, the accountability structure. When a team has a big vision and no roadmap, you build the roadmap. Your systems outlast you.

**Shadow:**
You can over-systematize things that don't need a system. You can resist flexibility even when the plan clearly needs to change. You may feel physical discomfort when things are disorganized or unfinished, and that discomfort can make you rigid with people who work differently.

**You at Your Best:**
Designing a workflow that turns a messy operation into a machine. Charting a 90-day plan with milestones, owners, and deadlines in one sitting. Closing open loops that have been hanging for weeks.

**You Under Stress:**
Becoming controlling when others don't follow your system. Spending more time organizing the work than doing the work. Getting paralyzed by open loops. Taking on other people's chaos because you can't stand watching it.

**What Others Should Know:**
"I need to know the sequence. Where are we, what's next, what's the end state? Give me that and I'll make sure we get there."

### QUICK START — How You Handle Risk, Change, and Uncertainty

---

#### QS-01: STABILIZE (Quick Start 1-3)

**Plain Title:** The Anchor

**Description:**
You bring calm to chaos. While others are chasing the next new thing, you're protecting what works. You don't resist change because you're afraid. You resist unnecessary change because you know the cost of breaking what's already functioning.

**Superpower:**
You're the reason the whole thing doesn't fall apart. You create standards. You hold the line. You're the one asking "have we tested this?" when everyone else is already halfway out the door. Teams without your wiring ship fast and break everything. Teams with you ship smart.

**Shadow:**
You can hold on too long. The status quo becomes a comfort zone, and comfort zones have a shelf life. You may resist innovation even when the current approach is clearly dying. The question to ask yourself isn't "is this stable?" It's "is this stable and still working?"

**You at Your Best:**
Preventing a costly mistake by asking the one cautious question nobody else thought to ask. Building standards that keep quality high under pressure. Being the person everyone trusts to not blow things up.

**You Under Stress:**
Saying no to everything new. Becoming the bottleneck because you won't greenlight anything without certainty. Feeling anxious when plans change suddenly. Digging into familiar routines even when they've stopped producing results.

**What Others Should Know:**
"I'm not against new ideas. I'm against untested ideas replacing things that already work. Convince me it's better and I'm in."

---

#### QS-02: MODIFY (Quick Start 4-6)

**Plain Title:** The Bridge

**Description:**
You live between stability and chaos. You'll try new things, but you test them first. You adjust deadlines, experiment with ideas that other people bring to the table, and create measured responses to new situations. You don't blow things up, and you don't freeze either.

**Superpower:**
You're the translator between the innovator and the stabilizer. You can take a wild idea and make it workable, or take a rigid process and make it flexible. You evaluate risk without being paralyzed by it or blind to it. You're the middle ground that keeps teams from tearing themselves apart.

**Shadow:**
Your flexibility can look like indecision. You may not push hard enough for change when it's needed, or push back hard enough against change when it's risky. The middle is powerful, but it can also mean you're waiting for someone else to commit first.

**You at Your Best:**
Taking someone's half-baked idea and shaping it into something that actually works. Adjusting a timeline when reality shifts, without losing the goal. Keeping a team moving when the innovator wants to pivot and the stabilizer wants to freeze.

**You Under Stress:**
Deferring to whoever is loudest in the room. Spending too long in "let's see" mode. Feeling torn between two strong opinions without anchoring to your own.

**What Others Should Know:**
"I'm open to change, but I want to understand the trade-offs first. Give me a reason to move and I'll move."

---

#### QS-03: INNOVATE (Quick Start 7-10)

**Plain Title:** The Spark

**Description:**
You are wired for risk. Not reckless risk. Creative risk. You generate ideas faster than most people can process them. You're energized by uncertainty, drawn to new challenges, and physically bored by anything you've done before. Repetition is your kryptonite. Possibility is your fuel.

**Superpower:**
You see what doesn't exist yet and feel compelled to build it. You're the person who walks into a stale situation and creates momentum out of thin air. You generate options when everyone else is stuck. You start things. That's rare and irreplaceable.

**Shadow:**
You start more than you finish. The thrill of the new idea is so strong that yesterday's idea loses its charge before it's done. You can destabilize teams by pivoting too fast. You may confuse activity with progress. "I started 10 things" is not the same as "I finished 1 thing."

**You at Your Best:**
Pitching the idea that changes the whole direction of the company. Solving an impossible problem by coming at it from an angle nobody considered. Energizing a demoralized team with sheer creative force. Improvising under pressure.

**You Under Stress:**
Starting new projects to avoid finishing old ones. Creating chaos that other people have to clean up. Getting bored with your own idea 48 hours after having it. Feeling trapped in any environment that demands repetition.

**What Others Should Know:**
"Let me brainstorm and prototype. Don't expect me to maintain the thing I built. Pair me with someone who loves finishing what I start."

---

### IMPLEMENTOR — How You Handle Physical Space and Tangible Things

---

#### IMP-01: ENVISION (Implementor 1-3)

**Plain Title:** The Abstract Thinker

**Description:**
You live in your head. Not because you're checked out, but because that's where your best work happens. You visualize outcomes, conceptualize solutions, and make decisions without needing to touch, build, or physically test anything. The picture in your mind is more real to you than a prototype on the table.

**Superpower:**
You can imagine finished products before a single piece has been assembled. You see the end state clearly while others are still figuring out the first step. You work fast because you don't need physical proof to trust your judgment. You're efficient because you skip the building stage entirely when possible.

**Shadow:**
You can undervalue hands-on work. You might dismiss the builder on the team because "I already see how it should look." You may also struggle to communicate your vision, because what's crystal clear in your head isn't visible to anyone else until they can see or touch it.

**You at Your Best:**
Making a complex decision without needing a prototype because you already see how it plays out. Describing a finished product so clearly that the team can build it without you. Cutting build time by resolving problems mentally before anyone touches material.

**You Under Stress:**
Refusing to engage with the physical world when a hands-on solution is required. Frustrating teammates by describing a vision you can see but can't make tangible. Avoiding physical tasks until someone else handles them.

**What Others Should Know:**
"I don't need to see the prototype. I already see it in my mind. But I know you might need to see it, so let me describe what I'm imagining."

---

#### IMP-02: RESTORE (Implementor 4-6)

**Plain Title:** The Fixer

**Description:**
You keep things working. Not by building from scratch, but by repairing, renovating, and upgrading what already exists. You extend the life of things. You see what's broken and feel a pull to make it right.

**Superpower:**
You're the person who walks into a situation and spots exactly what's failing. You fix it, improve it, and it works better than it did before. You don't need to reinvent the wheel. You just need to true the wheel that's already spinning. You're resourceful, practical, and efficient.

**Shadow:**
You might keep fixing something that should've been replaced. The instinct to restore can keep you patching a broken system long after a rebuild would have been faster. You can also undervalue your contribution because "fixing things" doesn't feel as impressive as "creating things."

**You at Your Best:**
Renovating a workspace, process, or product so it performs better than new. Spotting the broken piece in a machine (literal or metaphorical) that nobody else noticed. Extending the useful life of something valuable.

**You Under Stress:**
Fixing things compulsively instead of stepping back to ask if they should be fixed at all. Feeling drained when everything around you is broken and there's too much to repair. Holding onto things past their useful life.

**What Others Should Know:**
"Before we start over, let me look at what we've got. There might be more to save here than you think."

---

#### IMP-03: BUILD (Implementor 7-10)

**Plain Title:** The Maker

**Description:**
You need to touch it. Build it. Hold it. Your ideas become real when they exist in physical space. You think with your hands. Concepts that live only in documents or conversations feel incomplete to you until they take physical form.

**Superpower:**
You create tangible, durable things. You bring ideas out of the abstract and into reality. You craft, construct, and demonstrate. While others are still talking about what could be, you've already built a version of it. Your work is visible, holdable, and real. That carries a weight that documents never will.

**Shadow:**
You can over-invest in building before the concept is validated. You might build the prototype before the strategy is clear, which means rework. You can also dismiss ideas that don't have a physical component, which can make you undervalue strategic or abstract thinkers.

**You at Your Best:**
Building a working prototype while the team is still debating the concept. Demonstrating an idea so clearly that everyone instantly understands it. Crafting something with such quality that it speaks for itself.

**You Under Stress:**
Building things nobody asked for. Feeling useless in environments where nothing is physical. Getting frustrated in meetings because you'd rather be making something. Over-focusing on tangible output when the real problem is strategic.

**What Others Should Know:**
"Let me build something. Even a rough version. I understand things better when I can hold them. Don't make me sit in theory for too long."

## PART 2: COMBO PATTERN NARRATIVES

These describe what happens when two modes interact. Each person has 4 scores, so the most meaningful combos are the top 2 energy modes and the dominant/resistance tension.

---

### HIGH FF + LOW FT (Specifier + Adapter)
**The Deep Researcher Who Hates Process**
You'll research everything. Deeply. Thoroughly. But the moment someone asks you to put it in a spreadsheet with tabs and formulas, you check out. You need the freedom to go deep on information AND the flexibility to organize it your own way. Give you a blank doc, not a template.

### HIGH FF + HIGH FT (Specifier + Systematizer)
**The Architect**
You research deeply AND build structured systems. You're the person who creates the 90-day plan backed by real data. You're thorough and organized. The risk: you can over-engineer. You'll build a perfect system for a problem that needed a quick answer. Watch for diminishing returns on both depth and structure.

### HIGH FF + HIGH QS (Specifier + Innovator)
**The Strategic Instigator**
Dangerous combination. You research deeply AND move fast on new ideas. You don't guess. You gather evidence, then strike with conviction. The risk: you can use research as justification for risky bets, making them feel more certain than they actually are. Your confidence is earned, but it's not infallible.

### HIGH FF + LOW QS (Specifier + Stabilizer)
**The Careful Expert**
You go deep on information and prefer proven approaches. You're the most reliable analyst in the room. You won't present anything you haven't verified. The risk: you can become the "no" person. Your caution is valuable, but it can slow teams that need to move before the data is perfect.

### LOW FF + HIGH QS (Simplifier + Innovator)
**The Gut-Level Starter**
You don't need much data to act. You get the gist, trust your instinct, and go. You start things faster than almost anyone. The risk: you can build on a faulty premise because you didn't verify the foundation. Pair yourself with a Specifier who can stress-test your instincts.

### LOW FF + LOW QS (Simplifier + Stabilizer)
**The Practical Minimalist**
You want the bottom line, and you want it to be proven. You strip away complexity and stick with what works. You're efficient and reliable. The risk: you can miss opportunities because they require more investigation or more risk than you're comfortable with.

### HIGH FT + HIGH QS (Systematizer + Innovator)
**The Creative Operator**
You generate ideas AND build systems to execute them. Rare combination. You're the founder archetype. You can envision something new and actually operationalize it. The risk: internal tension. Your Systematizer wants to finish. Your Innovator wants to start something new. Managing that tension is your life's work.

### HIGH FT + LOW QS (Systematizer + Stabilizer)
**The Reliable Machine**
You build systems and protect the status quo. You're the person who creates the SOP and then follows it perfectly. Teams depend on you for consistency. The risk: rigidity. You can become so attached to your system that you resist change even when the system is outdated. Flexibility isn't your instinct, but it's your growth edge.

### LOW FT + HIGH QS (Adapter + Innovator)
**The Chaos Agent (Complimentary)**
You improvise, start fast, and resist any structure that slows you down. You generate massive creative energy but leave a wake of half-finished projects. You need a Systematizer on your team or you'll start 50 things and finish 3. Your gift is ignition. Your gap is completion.

### LOW FT + LOW QS (Adapter + Stabilizer)
**The Easy Operator**
You keep things simple and flexible. No unnecessary systems, no unnecessary risks. You go with the flow and adjust as needed. The risk: passivity. When neither change nor structure is your instinct, you can drift without direction. You need clear external signals (deadlines, accountability partners) to stay in motion.

### HIGH QS + HIGH IMP (Innovator + Builder)
**The Rapid Prototyper**
You come up with the idea and build it in the same breath. While others are still whiteboarding, you've got a working version. You're the startup in human form. The risk: you build before validating. Slow down long enough to confirm the idea is worth building before your hands take over.

### HIGH QS + LOW IMP (Innovator + Envisioner)
**The Idea Machine**
You generate ideas at extraordinary speed, and you see them fully formed in your mind. You don't need to build to understand. The risk: none of it becomes real unless someone else builds it. You need a Maker or a Builder on your team, or your best ideas stay imaginary.

### LOW QS + HIGH IMP (Stabilizer + Builder)
**The Craftsman**
You take proven concepts and build them beautifully. You don't chase novelty. You chase quality. You're the master of execution on things that already work. The risk: you can build the same thing over and over without evolving the concept. Innovation isn't your instinct, but small improvements compound.

### LOW QS + LOW IMP (Stabilizer + Envisioner)
**The Steady Thinker**
You process everything mentally. No need to build, no need to experiment. You think, evaluate, and decide. Quietly. Efficiently. The risk: invisible contribution. Others may not see your work because it happens internally. Speak up more, or your best thinking stays locked in your head.

### HIGH FF + HIGH IMP (Specifier + Builder)
**The Technical Craftsman**
You research deeply AND build tangible things. You're the engineer, the artisan, the person who knows exactly what to build and builds it to spec. The risk: perfectionism. You can spend forever getting both the research and the build exactly right. "Good enough" is a phrase you need to learn.

### HIGH FF + LOW IMP (Specifier + Envisioner)
**The Strategist**
You go deep on data and process everything mentally. You're the most thorough strategic thinker in the room. You don't need to touch it to trust it. You need to understand it. The risk: you can over-think. Your combination of deep research and abstract processing means you're always going one level deeper than necessary. At some point, the thinking has to become action.

### LOW FF + HIGH IMP (Simplifier + Builder)
**The Just-Do-It Builder**
You get the gist, skip the details, and start building. You produce tangible results faster than almost anyone. The risk: building the wrong thing. Without enough upfront data, your prototype might solve the wrong problem. Quick check-ins with a researcher can save you massive rework.

### LOW FF + LOW FT (Simplifier + Adapter)
**The Freestyler**
You want the headline, not the manual. And you definitely don't want to follow someone else's checklist. You're fast, flexible, and improvisational. The risk: lack of documentation. You solve problems and move on without leaving a trail. The next person who touches your work has no map.

### HIGH FT + HIGH IMP (Systematizer + Builder)
**The Operations Builder**
You design systems AND build the physical infrastructure to support them. You're the person who creates the process and the workspace. The risk: over-engineering the environment. Not everything needs a labeled drawer and a color-coded binder.

### HIGH FT + LOW IMP (Systematizer + Envisioner)
**The Systems Thinker**
You design complex processes entirely in your mind before implementing them digitally or on paper. You don't need physical tools. You need logic, sequence, and structure. The risk: your systems can feel abstract to people who need something tangible. Translate your mental models into something visual when working with others.

## PART 3: ENERGY DISTRIBUTION TEMPLATES

These narrative blocks describe what it means when a user's energy is concentrated in a specific mode.

---

### DOMINANT MODE NARRATIVES (Highest Energy %)

**Dominant Fact Finder:**
Your brain runs on information. Before you do anything, you need to understand it. This isn't overthinking. This is your engine. Starve it of data and you stall. Feed it the right inputs and you make decisions faster than anyone in the room, because you've already done the work.

**Dominant Follow Thru:**
Your brain runs on structure. Open loops drain you. Finished systems energize you. You feel most alive when something that was chaotic is now organized, sequenced, and running. This isn't being "Type A." This is your operating system. Respect it.

**Dominant Quick Start:**
Your brain runs on possibility. New ideas, new problems, new territory. The moment something becomes familiar, your energy drops. You need novelty the way other people need stability. Build your life around starting things, and pair yourself with people who love finishing them.

**Dominant Implementor:**
Your brain runs on the physical world. Ideas feel incomplete until you can touch them. You think with your hands. You understand by building. Environments that are all talk and no making will drain you faster than almost anything.

### RESISTANCE MODE NARRATIVES (Lowest Energy %)

**Resistant Fact Finder (Low FF):**
Deep research drains you. You can do it, but it costs more energy than it gives. Outsource the deep dives. Get the summary. Trust your pattern recognition to fill the gaps.

**Resistant Follow Thru (Low FT):**
Rigid process kills your momentum. You can follow a system when you have to, but it costs you. Build your own structure, make it flexible, and don't let anyone shame you for having "piles instead of files."

**Resistant Quick Start (Low QS):**
Uncertainty costs you energy. Ambiguity isn't exciting to you. It's exhausting. You perform best when the path is clear and the variables are known. There's nothing wrong with that. Not everyone needs to be a risk-taker.

**Resistant Implementor (Low IMP):**
Physical building isn't your instinct. You see it in your mind, you don't need to hold it in your hand. Hands-on tasks drain your tank. Delegate the tangible stuff and operate where your brain naturally works: in the abstract.

## PART 4: SECTION TEMPLATES (Operating Manual Sections Mapped to Conative Profiles)

Each section of the operating manual adapts based on the user's dominant mode and zone placements. Below are the template structures.

---

### SECTION: "How Your Brain Works"

**Template logic:** Lead with their dominant mode. Frame it as their primary operating frequency.

**Example for Dominant Quick Start:**
> Your brain is wired for what's next. New problems, new ideas, new challenges. The moment something becomes routine, your energy drops. This isn't a flaw. This is your frequency. The goal is to keep feeding it new inputs and building a life where starting things is your job, not a distraction from it.

**Example for Dominant Follow Thru:**
> Your brain is wired for order. Open loops, unfinished projects, disorganized systems. These don't just bother you. They drain your operating power. When things are organized and moving in sequence, you feel calm and productive. That's not being rigid. That's your engine running clean.

---

### SECTION: "What Success Looks Like"

**Template logic:** Map success definition to their top 2 energy modes.

**QS-dominant person's success:** Autonomy. Creative control. Variety. The ability to start new things without asking permission.

**FT-dominant person's success:** Completion. Systems that work. The satisfaction of seeing something go from messy to organized. Reliable outcomes.

**FF-dominant person's success:** Expertise. Being the person people come to because you know the most about the thing. Deep command of a domain.

**IMP-dominant person's success:** Tangible output. Something you can point at and say "I built that." Physical evidence of your work in the world.

---

### SECTION: "Danger Zones"

**Template logic:** Map danger zones to resistance modes and extreme scores.

**Low FF person's danger:** Making decisions on incomplete data and not realizing it until the consequences arrive.

**Low FT person's danger:** Promising to follow a process and then quietly doing it your own way, eroding trust without intending to.

**Low QS person's danger:** Holding on to something past its expiration because change feels like a threat rather than an opportunity.

**Low IMP person's danger:** Dismissing the value of physical prototypes, demos, or hands-on evidence when the situation genuinely requires it.

**High FF person's danger:** Researching until the window of opportunity closes.

**High FT person's danger:** Over-systematizing simple things. Building a process for something that needed a quick decision.

**High QS person's danger:** Destabilizing a team with too many pivots. Confusing activity with progress.

**High IMP person's danger:** Building before validating. Creating something beautiful that solves the wrong problem.

---

### SECTION: "Daily Operating Rules"

**Template logic:** Morning routine and work structure recommendations based on dominant mode.

**FF-Dominant Morning:** Start with 20 minutes of reading or research. Let your brain warm up on information before you're asked to produce.

**FT-Dominant Morning:** Review your plan for the day. Check your lists, confirm your sequence, close any open loops from yesterday. Then begin.

**QS-Dominant Morning:** Start with the thing that excites you. Not the responsible thing. The energizing thing. Let momentum carry you into the harder tasks.

**IMP-Dominant Morning:** Move your body first. Then build or make something with your hands. Physical engagement primes your whole system.

---

### SECTION: "Reset Protocols"

**Template logic:** What specifically brings each dominant mode back online when they're off.

**FF Reset:** Read something interesting. Research a new topic. Let your information-gathering instinct refuel you.

**FT Reset:** Organize something small. Clean a drawer, sort a folder, finish a tiny open loop. Completion restores your energy.

**QS Reset:** Do something new. Walk a different route, start a micro-project, brainstorm for 10 minutes. Novelty restarts your engine.

**IMP Reset:** Touch something physical. Cook. Build. Fix. Move your hands. Your brain reconnects through the physical world.

---

### SECTION: "Why You Procrastinate" (Mode-Specific Patterns)

**FF Procrastination Pattern:** You're stuck because you don't feel like you know enough to start. The fix isn't more research. It's accepting that you know enough right now and can course-correct later.

**FT Procrastination Pattern:** You're stuck because the task feels too disorganized to enter. The fix isn't a better system. It's defining one single next step and doing just that.

**QS Procrastination Pattern:** You're stuck because the task is boring. The fix isn't discipline. It's finding the angle that makes it interesting, or making it a race, or pairing it with something novel.

**IMP Procrastination Pattern:** You're stuck because the task is too abstract. The fix isn't more thinking. It's building something. Even a rough draft. Even a sketch. Get your hands on it.
